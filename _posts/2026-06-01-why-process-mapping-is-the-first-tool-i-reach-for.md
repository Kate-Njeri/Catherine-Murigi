Every operations problem I've been handed has arrived the same way: someone is unhappy with an outcome. Orders are late. A report takes too long. A vendor keeps missing a deadline. The instinct, especially for anyone comfortable with data, is to open a spreadsheet and start pulling numbers.

I've learned to resist that instinct for the first hour.

## Start with the path, not the data

A process map — even a rough one, drawn on a whiteboard with five boxes and some arrows — does something a query can't: it shows you where the handoffs are. Most operational failures don't happen inside a single step. They happen in the gap between two people or two systems, where nobody owns the outcome.

When I mapped the order fulfillment process for one of the case studies in this portfolio, the map itself wasn't impressive. Four boxes, four systems, a few arrows. But it made one thing obvious that no dashboard had surfaced in months: orders sat untouched for an average of six hours between the moment sales marked them "confirmed" and the moment the warehouse system actually saw them. That gap wasn't visible in any single system's reporting, because no single system owned both ends of it.

## The map tells you what to measure

This is the part that took me longest to internalize: the process map isn't a deliverable, it's a planning tool for the analysis that comes after. Once you can see the steps and the handoffs, you know exactly which timestamps matter. You stop pulling every column in the table and start pulling the three or four fields that actually explain the delay.

Skipping this step doesn't just waste time — it produces dashboards that are technically accurate and practically useless, because they measure what was easy to query instead of what was actually broken.

## A map is also a conversation starter

There's a quieter benefit too. Putting a process on a wall or a shared screen gives the people who actually do the work something concrete to react to. I've had warehouse leads correct a box in a map within ten seconds of seeing it — context I never would have gotten from a system export. Data tells you what happened. The people inside the process usually know why.

So the habit stays simple: map first, even badly. Query second. The map is what makes the query worth running.
